Customer demographics and lifestyle

As you learned on the previous page, it is very important for hospitality and catering organisations to understand customer preferences. This can help the organisation to ensure that the service they offer meets the particular needs and expectations of customers…which in turn leads to high levels of customer satisfaction.

Customer profiles can be used to help an organisation build up an understanding of who its customers are, what they do, how they live and what they need/expect from the organisation. Customer profiles can include the following information – click on the buttons to learn more:

  • Demographic information
  • Socioeconomic information
  • Psychographic information

Demographic information

Demographic information about customers allows you to understand characteristics such as:

  • Age
  • Gender
  • Marital/family status
  • Race and ethnicity
  • Education background
  • Income levels

Socioeconomic information

Socioeconomic information includes attributes related to:

  • Educational background
  • Household income levels
  • Employment/occupation

Psychographic information

Psychographic information about customers allows you to understand factors related to:

  • Lifestyle
  • Life stage
  • Personality
  • Attitudes
  • Values
  • Opinions
  • Interests

Establishing customer profiles can help the organisation to:

  • Understand its target customers
  • Understand what these customers need
  • Offer products/services that match the profiles of customers
  • Better meet the needs and expectations of customers, increasing levels of customer satisfaction, retention and loyalty
  • Market and promote products/services in a way that will appeal to customers

Examples

1. A fine dining restaurant in the City of London

Customers may be City workers employed in nearby businesses. They are likely to earn high salaries, meaning they have a lot of money to spend on dining out. They may also take clients to business dinners at the restaurant. They will enjoy high quality food and drinks, and expect a top level of service.

2. A pub near to University buildings in York

Students earning little or no income may be regular customers. They will be more interested in cheap, good value food and drink (quality will be a secondary consideration in their decision making). The pub can appeal to these customers with offers on food (buy one meal, get one free) and happy hour drinks.

3. A café in a leisure centre

The café may have a range of different customers to consider. For example:

  • Families using the swimming pool at the leisure centre may want quick, good value meals for their children, including child-friendly food and drink (such as sandwiches, fish fingers and chips, yoghurts, fruit and cartons of juice)
  • Health conscious individuals using the gym or other fitness facilities may want healthy salads or meals that are high in protein/low in fat
