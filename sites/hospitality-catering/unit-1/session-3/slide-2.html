Cost and profit

Preparing for your assessment

In your assessment for this Unit, you will need to be able to demonstrate that you are able to:

  • Explain factors affecting the success of hospitality and catering providers

You’ll find lots of useful information over the following pages to help you complete this part of your assessment.

Think first

Can you think of any factors, events or influences that could have either a positive or negative effect on the hospitality and catering industry? For example, think about things that could lead to an increase in popularity for hospitality/catering organisations. Alternatively, what changes could occur that would mean hospitality/catering organisations become less popular? Type some ideas in the box below before you move on.

As most hospitality and catering businesses are aiming to make money, one of the main success factors is all about how much money a business makes.

When a business operates, it will incur a range of costs. In order for a business to be financially successful, it needs to make enough money to cover these costs. Ideally, the business would aim to make more money than it spends – the money that remains in the business after covering all costs is known as profit. When a business makes a profit, it is able to use this money to invest in improving or growing the business, potentially making it more successful and therefore earning greater profits.

Business costs can be divided into two categories – fixed costs and variable costs. Click on the images below to learn more.

Fixed costs

These costs are not affected by the performance of the business – the business must pay these costs regardless of high or low customer numbers. For example, a business would need to pay the following fixed costs:

  • Rent or mortgage payments on buildings/premises
  • Wages for employees on fixed salaries
  • Insurance costs
  • Utility bills (electricity, water, gas, etc)

Variable costs

These costs will increase or decrease depending on the amount of business the organisation does. For example:

  • Wages would increase if the business needed to employ more staff to cover demand (similarly, they would decrease if fewer staff members were needed due to lack of customers)
  • Costs of ingredients (food and drink) and consumables (such as napkins, stationery and toiletries) would increase due to large customer numbers (or decrease if the business was not attracting many customers)

  • Chef’s wages
  • Wine
