Precautions when preparing food for people with food intolerances/allergies

When preparing meals for other people, it is important for a catering organisation to either be aware of any allergies or intolerances they may have and avoid ingredients that may trigger a reaction or clearly label dishes that may contain ingredients that may trigger a reaction so that customers suffering from allergies/intolerances can choose to avoid these dishes.

As well as avoiding serving certain foods to people with allergies or intolerances, catering businesses should also make sure that contamination does not occur during the process of preparing meals (ensuring that hands are washed after handling any ingredients known to cause allergies and using separate utensils, pans, etc).

Key point

Symptoms of food allergies can become life threatening very quickly. If you ever suspect that somebody eaten something they shouldn’t have and is experiencing anaphylactic shock (symptoms include breathing difficulties, a fast heartbeat, fainting, feeling or being sick, collapsing or losing consciousness), you should:

  1. Call 999 and ask for an ambulance. Tell the operator that you think the person has ‘anaphylaxis’.
  2. Lie the person down flat – unless they are unconscious, pregnant or experiencing breathing difficulties.
  3. Use an adrenaline auto-injector (such as an EpiPen) if the person has one and you know how to use it. People with known food allergies may carry one of these with them at all times in case of a reaction.
  4. If possible, give another injection after 5-15 minutes if the person has shown no signs of improvement (and the ambulance has not yet arrived).

It is important to remember that certain food-related health problems may have both visible and non-visible signs/symptoms. Take peanut allergies, for example – a person with a peanut allergy may display a range of visible and non-visible symptoms including:

Visible signs/symptoms:

  • Itching skin or hives
  • Difficulty speaking if airways begin to close
  • Runny nose, itchy/watering eyes
  • Swelling of the lips
  • Wheezing and coughing
  • Breathing difficulties, fainting, vomiting and/or loss consciousness, in cases of anaphylaxis

Non-visible signs/symptoms:

  • Blood pressure can drop severely (this is typically what causes of loss of consciousness)
  • Swelling of the tongue and/or throat
  • Stomach ache
  • Feelings of nausea
  • Increased heart rate
  • Tingling feelings in the lips or tongue
