Cross-contamination

One of the main reasons for maintaining safe and hygienic practice in a cooking environment is to ensure that harmful bacteria is not transferred from one area of food to another. This is called cross-contamination. This can cause food poisoning which in some instances can lead to very serious illness and even death in those that are vulnerable (such as the very young or the elderly). Food poisoning occurs when bacteria (such as clostridium perfingens or salmonella) are passed from raw meat onto other foods such as cooked dishes.

Bacteria need certain conditions in order to grow. One of the main factors in the growth of bacteria is temperature. Bacteria grow rapidly at 37°C, which is usually room or body temperature. Between 5 and 63°C, they continue to grow, although the rate at which they grow changes. This is known as the danger zone. At very cold temperatures, bacteria become dormant – they are not killed, but they do not grow or multiply.

Bacteria growth can be avoided through the use good personal hygiene, safe food storage, preparation and cooking techniques.
