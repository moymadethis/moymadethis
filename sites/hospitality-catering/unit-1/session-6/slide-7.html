Other customer rights

Customers visiting any food service establishment have the right to expect food that has been prepared and served in line with food safety legislation. The Food Safety Act 1990 sets out three main responsibilities for businesses – they must ensure that:

  1. They do not include or remove anything from food or treat food in a way that means it would be damaging to the health of those eating it
  2. Food served or sold is of the nature, substance or quality of which consumers would expect
  3. Food is labelled, advertised and presented in a way that isn’t false or misleading to others

This legislation ensures that customers have the right to expect food that is safe to eat and not hazardous to their health in any way.

Another piece of legislation which exists to protect customer rights is the Consumer Rights Act 2015.

Under this Act, any products/goods that are supplied to customers must:

  • Be of satisfactory quality
  • Be fit for purpose
  • Match the description, sample or model
  • Be installed correctly

Under this Act, any services that are supplied to customer must be:

  • Carried out with reasonable care and skill
  • Completed for a reasonable price
  • Completed within a reasonable time
  • Completed in accordance with any information said or conveyed in writing to the consumer where the consumer relies on it

Customer rights – examples

  1. If a customer books a table in a restaurant, they have the right to expect that table to be available on the day and time they have requested.
  2. If a customer doesn’t receive food of satisfactory quality, then they shouldn’t have to pay for it.
  3. If a customer doesn’t receive food that matches the description on the menu, then they shouldn’t have to pay for it.
  4. If a customer doesn’t receive good service in a restaurant, then they should not have to pay a service charge (tip).
  5. If a customer suffers food poisoning from a food establishment, then they should be able to claim a refund and/or compensation.
